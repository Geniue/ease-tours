<h2>The 90/180-Day Rule — Schengen Visa Duration</h2>
<p>One of the most important rules to understand:</p>
<p><strong>The Rule:</strong> You may stay in the Schengen Area for a maximum of <strong>90 days</strong> within any <strong>180-day</strong> rolling period.</p>
<p><strong>What does this mean in practice?</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The rule is <strong>rolling</strong> — not tied to a calendar year</li>
<li>Every day spent in the Schengen Area counts — including arrival and departure days</li>
<li>Once you've used all 90 days, you must wait until 180 days have passed from your first entry</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Practical example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You enter the Schengen Area on January 1st</li>
<li>You stay for 90 days (until March 31st)</li>
<li>You cannot re-enter until July 1st (180 days from January 1st)</li>
</ul>
<p>⚠️ <strong>Important:</strong> Overstaying beyond 90 days can result in fines, deportation, and a ban from the Schengen Area for up to 5 years!</p>

<h2>Where to Apply? (Embassy Selection Rule)</h2>
<p>One of the most common questions: <strong>Which embassy should I apply to?</strong></p>
<p>The rule is simple:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Visiting one country:</strong> Apply to that country's embassy</li>
<li><strong>Visiting multiple countries:</strong> Apply to the embassy of the country where you'll spend the <strong>longest time</strong></li>
<li><strong>Equal duration:</strong> Apply to the embassy of your <strong>first country of entry</strong></li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Visiting Paris (5 days) + Amsterdam (3 days) → Apply to the French embassy</li>
<li>Visiting Berlin (4 days) + Vienna (4 days), arriving in Berlin first → Apply to the German embassy</li>
</ul>
<p>⚠️ <strong>Warning:</strong> Applying to an embassy of a country you don't actually plan to visit (e.g., applying to Greece but going to France) can lead to rejection!</p>
